## COMMUNICATIVE FUNCTION

### Describing One's Own Experience

In describing his own experience, the writer does not only tell what happened to himself, but also what the experience felt like, or exactly how he behaved. Often in a detailed description of what happened to him the writer finds himself adding the feelings he had during the experience, the thoughts he has as he relives the event. In retrospect, the writer puts things together in order, and adds his later understanding.

### 2. Notes

> **[1]** **The author:** George Orwell (1903–1950), born in India, was sent to England at the age of eight for his education. Later he won a scholarship to Eton. There he was keenly aware of the differences between himself and his wealthy classmates. After graduation, he decided not to accept a scholarship to Oxford or Cambridge, but instead to serve the British Empire, at the age of 19, as an officer in the Indian Imperial Police in Burma. However, what he saw there soon disillusioned him.
>
> When the Spanish Civil War broke out in 1936, Orwell went to Spain to fight with the Republican Militia against the Fascist forces of Franco. He was seriously wounded. After he returned to England he worked for the BBC until 1943 when he became the literary editor of a left-wing Socialist newspaper. He wrote a great deal, and in 1945 he published *Animal Farm*, a political fable in which he used animals to represent different elements of a society under a totalitarian government. His last, bitter novel *Nineteen Eighty-Four* was published in 1949. Set in the future year 1984, the story portrays English society under an all-powerful dictator who oppresses and deceives the people.
>
> The present selection "Shooting an Elephant" is an essay based on Orwell's experience as an officer in the Indian Imperial Police in Burma. There he saw how much the Burmese hated being ruled by the British and found that he, as a white ruler, could never mingle or make friends with the Burmese people. And the incident itself, as he himself claimed, gave him a better glimpse than he had had before of the real nature of imperialism.

> **[2]** **Winchester:** A trademark for a shoulder firearm named after D. F. Winchester (1810–1880), US manufacturer. A Winchester rifle is a repeating rifle with a tubular magazine under the barrel, first made in about 1866.

> **[3]** ***in terrorem*:** as a warning

> **[4]** **but a tame one which had gone "must":** but a tame elephant which had become uncontrollable as compelled by instinct or urge within. "Must" refers to a temporarily wild and unruly state because of certain physiological factors.

> **[5]** **mahout:** keeper, driver or trainer of an elephant

> **[6]** **a black Dravidian coolie:** a coolie who was a member of any of the peoples that spoke one of the Dravidian languages, especially a member of the aboriginal population of southern India.
