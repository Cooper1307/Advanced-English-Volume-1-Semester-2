## Shooting an Elephant

射象

**By George Orwell**

乔治·奥威尔 著

One day something happened which in a roundabout way was enlightening. It was a tiny incident in itself, but it gave me a better glimpse than I had had before of the real nature of imperialism — the real motives for which despotic governments act. Early one morning the sub-inspector at a police station on the other end of town rang me up on the phone and said that an elephant was ravaging the bazaar. Would I please come and do something about it? I did not know what I could do, but I wanted to see what was happening and I got on a pony and started out. I took my rifle, an old .44 Winchester and much too small to kill an elephant, but I thought the noise might be useful *in terrorem*. Various Burmans stopped me on the way and told me about the elephant's doings. It was not, of course, a wild elephant, but a tame one which had gone "must." It had been chained up, as tame elephants always are when their attack of "must" is due, but on the previous night it had broken its chain and escaped. Its mahout, the only person who could manage it when it was in that state, had set out in pursuit, but had taken the wrong direction and was now twelve hours' journey away, and in the morning the elephant had suddenly reappeared in the town. The Burmese population had no weapons and were quite helpless against it. It had already destroyed somebody's bamboo hut, killed a cow and raided some fruit-stalls and devoured the stock; also it had met the municipal rubbish van and, when the driver jumped out and took to his heels, had turned the van over and inflicted violence upon it.

有一天发生了一件事，这件事以一种迂回的方式给了我启发。这件事本身只是一个小事件，但它让我比以前更清楚地看到了帝国主义的真实本质——专制政府行事的真正动机。一天清早，镇另一头的一个警察分局的副督察打电话给我，说有一头大象正在集市上横冲直撞，问我能不能去处理一下。我不知道自己能做什么，但还是想去看看到底怎么回事，便骑上一匹矮马出发了。我带了自己的步枪，一支老旧的点四四口径温彻斯特步枪，杀大象远远不够，但我心想，带上它或许能起个威慑（*in terrorem*）作用。路上好些缅甸人拦住我，告诉我那头大象的所作所为。当然，那不是一头野象，而是一头进入了"狂暴"状态的驯象。它原本是被拴着的——驯象在"狂暴"期到来时总是被拴起来——但头天晚上它挣断了铁链逃跑了。它的驯象人是唯一能在这种状态下控制它的人，已经出发去追赶，但走错了方向，现在已经在十二小时路程之外了。到了早上，大象突然又出现在镇上。缅甸人没有武器，面对它完全束手无策。它已经毁掉了某人的竹屋、踩死了一头牛、洗劫了几个水果摊并狼吞虎咽地吃光了存货；此外，它还碰上了市政垃圾车，当司机跳下车拔腿就跑后，它把车子掀翻，并对着它一顿猛砸。

The Burmese sub-inspector and some Indian constables were waiting for me in the quarter where the elephant had been seen. It was a very poor quarter, a labyrinth of squalid bamboo huts, thatched with palm-leaf, winding all over a steep hillside. I remember that it was a cloudy, stuffy morning at the beginning of the rains... . There was a loud, scandalized cry of "Go away, child! Go away this instant!" and an old woman with a switch in her hand came round the corner of a hut, violently shooing away a crowd of naked children. Some more women followed, clicking their tongues and exclaiming; evidently there was something that the children ought not to have seen. I rounded the hut and saw a man's dead body sprawling in the mud. He was an Indian, a black Dravidian coolie, almost naked, and he could not have been dead many minutes. The people said that the elephant had come suddenly upon him round the corner of the hut, caught him with its trunk, put its foot on his back and ground him into the earth. ... As soon as I saw the dead man I sent an orderly to a friend's house nearby to borrow an elephant rifle.

缅甸副督察和几名印度巡警正在大象出没的那个街区等我。那是一个非常贫穷的街区，一片由棕榈叶盖顶的破败竹棚屋所组成的迷宫，沿着一个陡峭的山坡蜿蜒分布。我记得那是雨季开始时一个阴沉闷热的早晨……突然传来一声气愤的大叫："走开，孩子！马上走开！"一个手拿树枝的老妇人从一间棚屋的拐角处走出来，粗暴地驱赶着一群光着身子的孩子。后面又跟来几个女人，啧啧连声地惊叫着；显然有什么东西是不该让孩子们看到的。我绕过棚屋，看到一具男尸毫无尊严地瘫倒在泥地里。他是一个印度人，一个黑皮肤的达罗毗荼苦力，几乎一丝不挂，死了应该还没几分钟。人们说大象从棚屋的拐角处突然冲到他面前，用鼻子卷住他，把脚踩在他背上，把他硬生生碾进了泥土里。……看到死者后，我立刻派了一名勤务兵到附近一个朋友家去借猎象步枪。

The orderly came back in a few minutes with a rifle and five cartridges, and meanwhile some Burmans had arrived and told us that the elephant was in the paddy fields below, only a few hundred yards away. As I started forward practically the whole population of the quarter flocked out of the houses and followed me. They had seen the rifle and were all shouting excitedly that I was going to shoot the elephant. They had not shown much interest in the elephant when he was merely ravaging their homes, but it was different now that he was going to be shot. It was a bit of fun to them, as it would be to an English crowd; besides they wanted the meat. It made me vaguely uneasy. I had no intention of shooting the elephant — I had merely sent for the rifle to defend myself if necessary — and it is always unnerving to have a crowd following you. I marched down the hill, looking and feeling a fool, with the rifle over my shoulder and an ever-growing army of people jostling at my heels. At the bottom, the elephant was standing eight yards from the road, his left side towards us. He took not the slightest notice of the crowd's approach. He was tearing up bunches of grass, beating them against his knees to clean them and stuffing them into his mouth.

勤务兵几分钟后就带着一支步枪和五发子弹回来了。与此同时，一些缅甸人赶来告诉我们大象就在下面的稻田里，只有几百码远。我刚一迈步，那个街区几乎全部的居民都从屋里涌了出来跟在我后面。他们看到了步枪，都兴奋地叫嚷着说我要去打大象了。当大象只是在糟蹋他们的家园时，他们并没有表现出多大兴趣，但现在大象要被打死了，情况就不同了。对他们来说这不过是个乐子，就像对英国人群一样；而且他们还想要象肉。这让我隐隐感到不安。我根本无意射杀大象——我只是派人取了步枪以便必要时自卫——而被一大群人跟着总是令人感到发毛。我扛着步枪走下山坡，由内而外地觉得自己像个小丑，身后是一支不断壮大的人群如影随形地推搡着我。到了山脚下，大象正站在离路八码远的地方，左侧面朝向我们。它对人群的靠近丝毫不以为意。它正在扯起一束束的草，在膝盖上拍打干净后塞进嘴里。

It was perfectly clear to me what I ought to do. I ought to walk up to within, say, twenty-five yards of the elephant and test his behavior. If he charged, I could shoot; if he took no notice of me, it would be safe to leave him until the mahout came back and caught him. Moreover, I did not in the least want to shoot him. I decided that I would watch him for a little while to make sure that he did not turn savage again, and then go home.

我非常清楚自己应该怎么做。我应该走到距大象大约二十五码左右的地方，观察它的反应。如果它冲过来，我就开枪；如果它对我不加理会，那就可以安全地把它留在那里，等驯象人回来把它收伏。而且我压根儿就不想打死它。我决定先观察它一会儿，确定它不会再次发狂，然后就回家。

But I did not want to shoot the elephant. I watched him beating his bunch of grass against his knees, with that preoccupied grandmotherly air that elephants have. It seemed to me that it would be murder to shoot him. At that age I was not squeamish about killing animals, but I had never shot an elephant and never wanted to. (Somehow it always seems worse to kill a large animal.) Besides, there was the beast's owner to be considered. Alive, the elephant was worth at least a hundred pounds; dead, he would only be worth the value of his tusks, five pounds, possibly.

但我并不想射杀这头大象。我看着它在膝盖上拍打着那束草，带着大象特有的那种全神贯注、老祖母般的神气。在我看来，射杀它无异于谋杀。在那个年纪，我对于杀害动物并不会感到于心不忍，但我从未射杀过大象，也从没想过要这么做。（不知怎么的，杀死一只庞大的动物似乎总是显得更为恶劣。）此外，还得考虑这头野兽的主人。活着的话，这头大象至少值一百英镑；死了的话，它就只值它象牙的价钱了，也许只有五英镑。

But at that moment, I glanced round at the crowd that had followed me. It was an immense crowd, two thousand at the least and growing every minute. It blocked the road for a long distance on either side. I looked at the sea of yellow faces above the garish clothes — faces all happy and excited over this bit of fun, all certain that the elephant was going to be shot. They were watching me as they would watch a conjuror about to perform a trick. They did not like me, but with the magical rifle in my hands I was momentarily worth watching. And suddenly I realized that I should have to shoot the elephant after all. The people expected it of me and I had got to do it; I could feel their two thousand wills pressing me forward, irresistibly. And it was at this moment, as I stood there with the rifle in my hands, that I first grasped the hollowness, the futility of the white man's dominion in the East. Here was I, the white man with his gun, standing in front of the unarmed native crowd — seemingly the leading actor of the piece; but in reality I was only an absurd puppet pushed to and fro by the will of those yellow faces behind. I perceived in this moment that when the white man turns tyrant it is his own freedom that he destroys. He becomes a sort of hollow, posing dummy, the conventionalized figure of a sahib. For it is the condition of his rule that he shall spend his life in trying to impress the "natives," and so in every crisis he has got to do what the "natives" expect of him. He wears a mask, and his face grows to fit it. I had got to shoot the elephant. I had committed myself to doing it when I sent for the rifle. A sahib has got to act like a sahib; he has got to appear resolute, to know his own mind and do definite things. To come all that way, rifle in hand, with two thousand people marching at my heels, and then to trail feebly away, having done nothing — no, that was impossible. The crowd would laugh at me. And my whole life, every white man's life in the East, was one long struggle not to be laughed at.

但就在那一刻，我回头看了看跟在我身后的人群。那是一大群人，至少有两千人，而且每分钟都在增加。人群把两边的路堵了很长一段距离。我望着花花绿绿的衣服上方那一片黄色面孔的海洋——所有的面孔都为了看这出好戏而兴奋不已，都坚信大象将被射杀。他们看着我，就像看一个即将表演戏法的魔术师。他们并不喜欢我，但凭着手中那神奇的步枪，我暂时值得一看。突然间我意识到，我终究还是得打那头大象。人们期望我这样做，而我不得不这样做；我能感觉到两千人的意志势不可挡地推着我向前。正是在这一刻，当我手持步枪站在那里时，我第一次领悟到白人在东方统治的空洞和徒劳。看看我吧，白人手持枪支，站在手无寸铁的本地人群面前——表面上是这出戏的主角；但实际上我不过是一个荒唐的木偶，被身后那些黄色面孔的意志随意摆弄。在那一刻我认识到，当白人变成暴君时，他毁掉的是自己的自由。他变成了一种空洞的、摆姿态的假人，一个套路化的"老爷"（sahib）形象。因为他统治的条件就是：他必须一辈子都在设法让"土著人"佩服自己，因而在每一个危急关头，他都不得不按"土著人"所期望的去做。他戴着一副面具，久而久之脸也长成了面具的形状。我不得不去射杀那头大象。当我派人去取步枪的那一刻，我就已经把自己逼上了绝路。一个老爷就得像个老爷的样子；他必须显得果断，理清自己的主意，做出明确的行动。大老远地拿着步枪来了，两千人浩浩荡荡跟在身后，然后灰溜溜地空手而归——不，那是不可能的。人群会嘲笑我的。而我的全部生活，每一个白人在东方的生活，就是一场漫长的为了避免被嘲笑的斗争。

...

……

I had halted on the road. As soon as I saw the elephant I knew with perfect certainty that I ought not to shoot him. It is a serious matter to shoot a working elephant — it is comparable to destroying a huge and costly piece of machinery — and obviously one ought not to do it if it can possibly be avoided. And at that distance, peacefully eating, the elephant looked no more dangerous than a cow. I thought then and I think now that his attack of "must" was already passing off; in which case he would merely wander harmlessly about until the mahout came back. But also I knew that I was going to do no such thing. I was a poor shot with a rifle and the ground was soft mud into which one would sink at every step. If the elephant charged and I missed him, I should have about as much chance as a toad under a steam-roller. But even then I was not thinking particularly of my own skin, only of the watchful yellow faces behind. For at that moment, with the crowd watching me, I was not afraid in the ordinary sense, as I would have been if I had been alone. A white man mustn't be frightened in front of "natives"; and so, in general, he isn't frightened. The sole thought in my mind was that if anything went wrong those two thousand Burmans would see me pursued, caught, trampled on and reduced to a grinning corpse like that Indian up the hill. And if that happened it was quite probable that some of them would laugh. That would never do. There was only one alternative. I shoved the cartridges into the magazine and lay down on the road to get a better aim.

我已经在路上停下了脚步。我一看到大象便完全确信我不应该射杀它。射杀一头做工的大象是一件严重的事——这好比是毁掉一台巨大而昂贵的机器——如果可以避免，显然就不该这样做。在那个距离看去，大象正平静地吃着草，看上去跟一头牛没什么两样。当时我就想，现在我仍然这么想，它的"狂暴"发作已经在消退了；在那种情况下，它只会无害地四处游荡，直到驯象人回来。但是我也清楚，我绝不可能那么做。我步枪打得极烂，而脚下是松软的泥地，每走一步都会陷下去。如果大象冲过来而我又没打中，恐怕就像压路机下的癞蛤蟆一样，只能粉身碎骨了。然而即便如此，我也并非特别担心自己的性命，我在意的只是身后那些警觉的黄色面孔。因为在那一刻，被人群注视着，我并不是在通常意义上感到害怕——如果我是独自一人，那才会害怕。白人不能在"土著人"面前显出害怕的样子；所以一般来说，他也确实不会害怕。我脑子里唯一的念头就是：万一出了岔子，那两千个缅甸人就会看到我被追赶、被抓住、被踩踏，变成一具像山上那个印度人一样咧嘴而笑的尸体。而如果真发生了那样的事，他们中多半就会有人发笑。那样的场面是绝不允许发生的。只有一条路可走了。我把子弹推进弹仓，趴在路上以便更好地瞄准。

...

……

Afterwards, of course, there were endless discussions about the shooting of the elephant. The owner was furious, but he was only an Indian and could do nothing. Besides, legally I had done the right thing, for a mad elephant has to be killed, like a mad dog, if its owner fails to control it. Among the Europeans opinion was divided. The older men said I was right, the younger men said it was a damn shame to shoot an elephant for killing a coolie, because the elephant was worth more than any damn Coringhee coolie. And afterwards I was very glad that the coolie had been killed; it put me legally in the right and it gave me sufficient pretext for shooting the elephant. I often wondered whether any of the others grasped that I had done it solely to avoid looking a fool.

事后，当然免不了没完没了地讨论这次射象事件。象的主人勃然大怒，但他只是一个印度人，无能为力。何况从法律上说，我做的是对的——一头发了狂的大象必须被射杀，就像一只疯狗一样，只要它的主人无法控制它。在欧洲人当中意见不一。年长的人说我做得对；年轻的人说为了打死一个苦力就射杀一头大象太不像话了，因为大象比任何一个该死的科林吉苦力都值钱得多。而后来我倒是很庆幸那个苦力被踩死了；这使我在法律上站住了脚，也给了我射杀大象的充足借口。我常常想，不知道其他人中是否有人看出我这样做纯粹只是为了不让自己显得像个傻瓜。

## 3. Comprehension and Discussion Questions

3\. 理解与讨论问题

1. What was it that was reported to be happening one day?

   据报道那天发生了什么事？

2. Why was the author involved in the event?

   作者为什么会卷入这一事件？

3. How did he feel about it?

   他对此事有何感受？

4. The entire selection seems to be telling a story; but what is the significance of the story? And where is it told?

   整篇选文似乎在讲述一个故事；但这个故事的意义何在？又是从哪里点明的？

5. Why did the author finally shoot the elephant? Where does he tell his reasons for shooting the elephant and what is its significance?

   作者最终为什么射杀了大象？他在哪里交代了射杀大象的理由？其意义是什么？
